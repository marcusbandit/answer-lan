How Tsunamis Work

A tsunami is a series of ocean waves triggered by sudden, large-scale displacement of water, usually from underwater earthquakes, but also from landslides, volcanic eruptions, or meteorites. In deep water these waves travel at jet speeds of up to 800 km per hour with wavelengths over 500 km but wave heights of only 30 to 60 cm, making them barely noticeable, before slowing down and rising into destructive walls of water as they reach shore.

The Step-by-Step Process

  1. Displacement: An underwater earthquake or similar sudden event displaces a large volume of ocean water, pushing it up or down from its normal level.
  2. Wave Generation: The displaced water creates waves that radiate outward in all directions, forming what's called a wave train, a series of connected ocean waves.
  3. Deep Water Travel: In the open ocean, tsunami waves travel at speeds up to 800 km per hour with wavelengths exceeding 500 km, but with wave heights of only 30 to 60 cm, making them nearly invisible to ships.
  4. Shallow Water Rise: As the waves approach shore and the ocean floor becomes shallower, the waves slow down while the water behind piles up, causing the wave height to increase dramatically.
  5. Destructive Impact: The tsunami arrives as one or more surging walls of water that can inundate coastal communities and reshape coastlines, causing catastrophic damage.
